## Needs vs Wants

Module: `budgeting` / "Needs vs Wants" (icon 🛒, free module, order_index 4).

These are the draft **Level 2** (free) and **Level 3** (premium) lessons that extend the
existing Level 1. The focus stays on **smart-spending decision-making** — value, delayed
gratification, advertising and peer pressure, resisting hype — and deliberately avoids
budgeting *mechanics* (the 50/30/20 split, surplus/deficit, tracking maths) which live in
the separate "Budgeting Basics" module.

### How this builds beyond Level 1
- **L1** taught the *definition* (the blurry need/want line), simple sorting examples, that
  small treats add up, and two "needs come first" trade-off scenarios.
- **L2 (Smart choosing: tools for a better decision)** gives kids practical decision tools —
  the wait-a-bit test, cost-per-use / value thinking, opportunity cost ("what does this
  *really* cost me?"), and price-vs-worth — so they can *make* the call, not just label it.
- **L3 (Resisting the pull: ads, hype & peer pressure)** is mastery: spotting how
  advertising, influencers, "limited time!" tricks and friends nudge wants, and staying calm
  and in control (including asking a trusted grown-up).

## Level 2 — Smart choosing (free)

### Card 1 — The "wait a bit" test
When you really want something, try the **wait-a-bit test**: give yourself a short pause —
a day, or even just a sleep on it — before buying. Lots of wants feel huge in the moment and
much smaller the next day. If you still want it after waiting, it's probably a genuine choice,
not just an impulse. Waiting costs you nothing and often saves you money.

### Card 2 — Cost per use: the real value
Price isn't the same as value. A £40 pair of shoes you wear every day can be *better value*
than a £15 pair you wear twice. A handy trick is **cost per use**: roughly, the price divided
by how many times you'll actually use it. Something cheap that you never use is expensive in
disguise.

### Quiz 1
**Q:** What is the "wait a bit" test?
- A basic phone you must have for school
- Giving yourself a short pause before buying, to check it's not just an impulse ✅
- Buying something only if it's on sale
- Asking a shop to lower the price

**Explanation:** Pausing before you buy lets the first rush of "I want it NOW" fade, so you
can decide calmly. If you still want it after waiting, that's a real choice.

### Quiz 2
**Q:** £40 boots you'll wear 200 times, or £15 boots you'll wear 5 times. Which is better
*value* (cost per use)?
- The £15 boots, because they cost less
- The £40 boots — about 20p per wear vs £3 per wear ✅
- They're exactly the same value
- You can't compare them

**Explanation:** £40 ÷ 200 ≈ 20p each time; £15 ÷ 5 = £3 each time. The pricier boots are far
better value because you'll actually use them. Cheap-but-unused is expensive in disguise.

### Quiz 3
**Q:** You spend your last £20 on a game instead of a cinema trip with friends. The cinema
trip you gave up is called the…
- Bonus
- Opportunity cost ✅
- Refund
- Interest

**Explanation:** Every choice has an **opportunity cost** — the next-best thing you gave up.
Money spent once can't be spent again, so it's worth asking "what am I saying no to?"

### Quiz 4
**Q:** A toy is reduced from £50 to £30. Which question best tells you if it's a smart buy?
- "How big is the discount?"
- "Would I want this and use it even at £30 if it had never been £50?" ✅
- "Will it sell out today?"
- "What colour is it?"

**Explanation:** A discount only saves money if you'd genuinely want and use the thing
anyway. "It's 40% off!" isn't a reason to buy — the real question is whether it's worth £30
*to you*.

### Scenario
**Prompt:** You see a £35 gadget online and you *really* want it right now. You've got the
money. What's the smartest move?
- **Add it to a wishlist and check again in a few days** → Great use of the wait-a-bit test.
  If you still want it later, you can buy it as a calm choice — and you'll often find the urge
  has faded. ✅ *(correct)*
- **Buy it instantly before the feeling passes** → That rush is exactly what impulse buying
  feels like. Buying before you've thought it through is how wants win over sensible choices.
- **Buy two in case one breaks** → That's spending even more on an impulse you haven't tested.
  One is already an unplanned want; two doubles the regret risk.

**Correct:** option 1 (index 0).

## Level 3 — Resisting the pull: ads, hype & peer pressure (premium)

### Card 1 — Adverts are made to make you want
Adverts, sponsored posts and influencer videos exist to make you *feel* you need something.
Bright colours, happy faces, "everyone has it" — these are clever tools, not facts. Spotting
that an advert is *designed* to nudge you is a superpower: once you see the trick, it has far
less power over you. Wanting something because an advert told you to isn't the same as
actually needing it.

### Card 2 — "Limited time!" and other hurry-up tricks
"Only 2 left!", "Sale ends tonight!", "Don't miss out!" — these messages are built to make
you panic-buy before you've had time to think. Real needs don't have a countdown timer. When
something is shouting at you to hurry, that's exactly the moment to slow down, breathe, and
ask a trusted grown-up if you're unsure.

### Quiz 1
**Q:** What is the main job of an advert?
- To give you honest, balanced advice
- To make you *want* the product and feel you should buy it ✅
- To save you money on purpose
- To tell you what you genuinely need

**Explanation:** Adverts are made to sell, not to advise. Knowing that helps you enjoy them
without letting them decide your spending for you.

### Quiz 2
**Q:** A site flashes "Only 1 left — sale ends in 5 minutes!" What is this mostly designed to
do?
- Help you make a calm, careful decision
- Rush you into buying before you've thought it through ✅
- Give you a guaranteed best price
- Remind you of something you truly need

**Explanation:** Countdown timers and "almost gone!" messages create false panic. A genuine
need doesn't expire in five minutes — so that pressure is your cue to slow right down.

### Quiz 3
**Q:** Your friends all bought the same trainers and say you "have to" get them too. That
pressure to want what the group wants is called…
- Opportunity cost
- Peer pressure ✅
- Compound interest
- A refund

**Explanation:** Wanting something just because friends have it is **peer pressure**. It's
normal to feel it — but it's your money and your choice. Real friends won't mind if you spend
differently.

### Quiz 4
**Q:** A favourite influencer is paid to show off a £60 hoodie. What's the wisest way to think
about it?
- "They love it, so I obviously need it"
- "They're being paid to promote it, so I'll decide for myself if it's worth £60 to me" ✅
- "Everything they recommend must be a great deal"
- "I should buy it fast before it sells out"

**Explanation:** Sponsored posts are paid adverts. The person may be lovely, but the question
is still whether the item is worth the price *to you* — not whether someone famous showed it.

### Scenario
**Prompt:** A big online sale is on. An influencer you follow is hyping a £45 jacket, the page
says "Only a few left!", and two friends have already bought it. You've got £45 saved for
something else. What do you do?
- **Buy it right now so you don't miss out or feel left out** → That's ads, hype and peer
  pressure all pulling at once — the exact mix designed to make you spend without thinking.
- **Pause, remember it's all designed to rush you, and chat to a trusted grown-up before
  deciding** → Spot-on. Naming the pressure (advert + countdown + friends) takes its power
  away, and a trusted grown-up can help you decide calmly. ✅ *(correct)*
- **Borrow money so you can buy it AND keep your £45** → Borrowing for a hyped-up want is how
  small wants turn into money you owe. Never take on debt because of a countdown timer.

**Correct:** option 2 (index 1).
